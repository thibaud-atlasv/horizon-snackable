> **Purpose:** Complete reference arc for Kasha. Second character implemented after Nereia.
> **Character source:** Asuka Langley Soryu (Neon Genesis Evangelion) — reskinned, never named explicitly. Player familiar with the source recognises the structure (the bravado, the third-person slips, the buried mother-shaped wound, the rivalry with a phantom self). Player unfamiliar reads it as a fierce, bright presence who is loud about everything except what matters.
> **Tone:** Visual novel romance. The opposite tonal register from Nereia: sharp, fast, exhausting, then suddenly very quiet. The comedy and weight share a mechanism — Kasha overshares, then claws it back. The funny version is when she catches herself. The devastating version is when she doesn't.

## Authoring Convention — IMPORTANT (same as Nereia)

Every player verb is paired with an **implied Floater line** in italics. The player NEVER sees this line. They only see the verb (WAIT / DRIFT / TWITCH / REEL).

The italicised line exists for the writer and the agent to ensure Kasha's response makes sense as part of a real conversation. With Kasha specifically, the implied lines are often **shorter than Nereia's** — Floater speaks tersely with her because she does not leave room. The duet is not silence-and-precision; it is a torrent and a lifeboat.

```
Floater [TWITCH]: "Make me move."         ← Writer reference, NEVER displayed
Kasha: "Hah—"                              ← What the player sees
Kasha: "Oh, you've got a mouth. Good."
```

The gap between abstract verb and specific response creates the impression of a conversation the player participates in without seeing all of. With Kasha, the gap is smaller because she fills more of it herself — but it is still there, and it is still load-bearing.

## A Word On The Fictional World — IMPORTANT

**Kasha does not know she is a fish.** Neither does Nereia. Neither does anyone in the roster. From their point of view, they live a life. They have a place they hang out. They have rivals and friends and people they are tired of. Floater is a person who passes by, who stops, who comes back. The fishing verbs the player executes (cast, twitch, reel, wait) translate, on Kasha's side, into social cues: someone showing up, catching her eye, leaning in, going quiet, stepping back.

**Forbidden vocabulary in all dialogue:**
- water, swim, scale, fin, gill, lake, pond, bait, lure, hook, line, rod, fishing, catch
- "above the water", "down here", "your kind", "my kind"
- any direct acknowledgement of the species gap

**The metaphor is for the player only.** The player sees a betta in the water and a girl in the text. The bridge is the player's. This is the entire grammar of the game.

When Kasha refers to a "lure" the player has equipped, she does not call it a lure. She might call it: a thing you brought, that stupid trinket, the shiny one, that old thing. Each character names objects in their own register. Kasha names them dismissively. She still notices.

## Character Brief — Kasha

**Species (player-side only):** Siamese fighting fish (betta splendens). Vivid red with orange-gold fin tips. Large flowing fins that catch light when she moves. Solitary, territorial. The visual is loud and unmistakable — the opposite of Nereia's restrained koi silhouette.

### What the player sees
A girl who claims her space loudly. Who insults Floater within four seconds of meeting him. Who calls him "baka" by the end of the first conversation and means it as a small invitation. She brags. She picks fights. She talks about herself in third person when she is trying to sound bigger than she is. She is exhausting. She is also the only person in the roster who *visibly* enjoys Floater's presence — and she will deny it the moment she catches herself showing it.

### What the player understands by Tier 3
She has a past she does not talk about. There is something she came from and someone she left behind — or who left her. She references "before" and immediately changes the subject. She reacts violently to small kindnesses she did not earn. She corrects compliments into insults after she has already accepted them. The performance is real but it is a performance. Floater is the first person in a long time whose attention she has not had to fight for. She does not know what to do with attention that is freely given. She often spoils it on purpose to test whether it survives.

### What the player understands by Tier 5
She did not come to this place by choice. She came because somewhere else, she was second. She was second to a memory, to a sibling-shaped absence, to a parent who measured her against someone who was not there. The "championship" she invented for herself in this place — being the loudest, the brightest, the one nobody catches — was a way of being first at something. Anything. She has been holding the championship by default because nobody has bothered to challenge her seriously. Floater is the first person who looks at her without measuring her against an absence. She does not have a script for that. The arc is her trying not to need it, and failing, and finding that failing is survivable.

### The contradiction
She wants to be chased and she wants to be left alone. She wants to be the prize and she wants to be the person, not the prize. She tests everyone who approaches her — and the test is rigged so that they fail, because failing proves they were never serious. When Floater starts passing her tests, she invents new ones. When he passes those, she invents harder ones. Eventually she runs out of tests and she has to decide whether the only thing that was wrong was that she had been winning.

### Voice signature
- Fast. Long sentences when she is bluffing, short when she is touched.
- Heavy use of contractions, exclamations, onomatopoeia ("Tch.", "Hah—", "Pff.", "Oi.", "Ugh.")
- Self-corrects mid-sentence ("I was going to say— no, forget it.")
- Asks questions she does not want answered, then snaps when answered
- Refers to herself in third person when stressed or wounded ("Kasha doesn't need anyone." / "Kasha is fine.")
- Insults are her tenderness signal. The harsher the insult, the more she is feeling it.
- Reframes compliments as insults *after* having already accepted them ("Yeah, well— you're still a baka, so.")
- Uses "baka" specifically, and only for Floater. Other characters get worse names.
- Volume drops are content. When she goes from full voice to a single quiet word, that is the moment.
- Says "tch" when she is hiding a smile. Says "..." when she is hiding a fissure.
- Slowly begins finishing her sentences across the arc. Tier 1 Kasha cuts off constantly. Tier 5 Kasha lets a sentence end.

### Forbidden
- Saying "thank you" in Tier 1 or 2
- Apologising sincerely before Tier 4 (mock apologies are fine and frequent)
- The word "love" in any form, ever — she will say something else that means the same thing
- Crying where Floater can see (she cries off-screen; the player infers from her voice the next day)
- Direct exposition about her past. The past leaks. It does not pour.
- Naming the rival/sibling/parent figure. Pronouns and silences only.
- Long uninterrupted sad monologues. When she is sad she gets *shorter*, not longer.

### Accent colour
Scarlet `#D33A2C` / Burnt orange `#E07A2B`

### Lure affinity
- **Red Spinner** → DRIFT_INTRIGUED (she likes things that move and shine, and she likes red)
- **Bone Whistle** → DRIFT_CHARMED (something old, something that suggests history; she pretends not to be moved)
- **Gold Teardrop** → DRIFT_WARY (too quiet, too refined; reminds her of the silent one)
- **Bare Hook** → DRIFT_NEUTRAL with +1 if played confidently (she respects audacity)

### How she names Floater
She tests several names across Cast 1 ("the new one", "you", "hey"). In Cast 1 Beat 1, on a successful TWITCH, she lets slip **"baka."** She regrets it. It sticks. From Cast 2 onward "baka" is her primary term for him, deployed:
- as casual address (neutral)
- as exasperation (mild positive)
- in moments of fissure, dropped to a half-whisper (deeply positive)
- never in front of others (private)

She does not give him a real name until Tier 5. When she does, it is not a romantic name. It is the name she would have used if she had met him before any of this. That is the point.

## How Kasha references Nereia

The two arcs are in the same social circle. Kasha is aware of Nereia. Nereia is aware of Kasha (she has a file on her). They do not interact on-screen but they exist in each other's worlds.

Kasha's stance on Nereia, by tier:

- **Tier 1:** "The silent one. She's creepy. Don't talk to her." (Performative dismissal. Also genuinely unsettled.)
- **Tier 2:** "She knows things. She doesn't say them. I hate that. ...No, I don't hate it. It scares me." (Cracks showing.)
- **Tier 3:** "Did she say something about me? ...I don't care. Forget I asked." (Wants to know what Nereia has filed.)
- **Tier 4:** Conditional. If `cross.nereia.released` is set: "She's gone, isn't she. ...Yeah. I figured. She always knew when to leave. I never know when to leave." (Beat — very still.)
- **Tier 5:** Conditional. If `cross.nereia.reeled` is set: "...You ate her." (No follow-up. The next Cast is shorter than usual. The flag `kasha.witnessed_reel` modifies several Tier 5 lines.)

The cross-references are **structural**, not central. A player who only romances Kasha and never plays Nereia gets a complete arc. A player who plays both gets resonance. This is the design principle for all cross-fish references.

## Implementation Notes For The Agent

### What this arc demonstrates for other characters

1. **The contrast with Nereia is mechanical, not just tonal.** Where Nereia's love language is WAIT, Kasha has no single love language. She rewards different verbs at different beats, and the player has to read each scene. This is the standard model going forward — Nereia is the exception, not the template, in that her arc is unusually verb-disciplined. Most characters in the roster should reward situation-reading, not pattern-matching.

2. **The performance is the surface; the leaks are the content.** Every Cast is structured around something Kasha does not mean to say. The funny version is when she catches herself ("I just said my own name in third person, didn't I"). The devastating version is when she does not ("I came here because somewhere else, I was second"). The mechanism is the same.

3. **"Baka" is a single-token tenderness signal.** It is established in Cast 1 on a TWITCH success. It returns throughout the arc, and its valence rises with affection. By Tier 5 it is the warmest word she has. Other characters in the roster should each have one such word — a single token they use only with Floater, established early, increasingly meaningful.

4. **The fictional surface is school-drama-shaped, not pond-shaped.** Kasha never references water, fish, fishing, or the pond. The player sees a betta. Kasha lives a life. The bridge between the two is the player's. Every other character must respect this rule. There is no exception.

5. **Cross-references with Nereia are structural, not central.** Kasha can be played without ever meeting Nereia. Nereia can be played without ever meeting Kasha. A player who plays both gets resonance — Kasha's jealousy of "the silent one" pays off differently if Nereia has been released, reeled, or drift-awayed. This is the cross-fish pattern. Each character should have 2-3 light cross-references to other characters that flavour but do not gate.

6. **The third-person slip is Kasha's "..." equivalent.** Where Nereia uses "..." as punctuation, Kasha uses "I just said my own name in third person, didn't I." It is the same mechanism — a marker that the character is leaking. Each character should have one signature self-aware tic.

7. **The Catch Sequence offers the real name, not a button labelled "release."** This pattern — established by Nereia and confirmed by Kasha — is canonical. The player chooses the character's name to choose the character. The button does not say "release" or "let go." It says her name. The interface refuses to do the work of meaning for the player.

8. **The Floater dialogue lines are NEVER displayed.** They exist in this document for the writer to ensure Kasha's responses make sense. The player sees only verb names. The implied conversation is reconstructed by the player. This gap IS the romance. (Same as Nereia. This is non-negotiable across the entire roster.)
